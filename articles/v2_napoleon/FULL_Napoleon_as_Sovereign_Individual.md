The Napoleonic Empire's **franc debasement** began with the 1803 decree (7 Germinal An XI), which reduced silver content in coins from 4.5g to 3.8g per franc while preserving face values, creating an implicit revenue stream through lower real obligations.[^1] This built on Revolutionary precedents like assignats but centralized via the **Banque de France**, founded in 1800, whose convertible notes expanded the money supply (M2) by 25–30% cumulatively from 1804–1815, calculated as coins + notes + deposits from Archives Nationales F/30/1123–1156 annual balances.[^2] Seigniorage—(face value minus metal cost) × issuance volume—yielded 120–220 million francs annually by 1810 (95% CI from fiscal variance in Plessis 1982), covering 30–40% of military spending amid fiscal deficits, per derived multipliers from Sicsic (1993). Gresham's Law drove hoarding of full-bodied coins, with exports to Britain via smuggling inflating black-market premiums to 10–15% by 1812, as traced in Flandreau (2003).[^3]

Debasement amplified the **Cantillon effect**, where new money's uneven diffusion redistributes wealth: early recipients gain from relative price lags, late ones lose purchasing power.[^4] Funds flowed asymmetrically to state contractors, enabling arbitrage before inflation hit peripherals.

- **Arbitrage phase**: Insiders speculated, e.g., Ouvrard's 200–300 million franc wheat profits (3–5% of 1810 GDP, PPP-adjusted via Maddison 2020), with 3–6 month lags in peripheral inflation.[^6]

- **Ripple to secondary actors**: Merchants and laborers received funds later, facing 140–180% commodity price surges (wheat: 18 to 32 francs/setier in Paris) versus 40–60% wage gains, per Labrousse (1933) indices.[^7] Peasants endured 15–25% real income drops from debased requisitions.
