# Tribute Empires vs Productive Empires: Rome, Spain, France

The **tribute empires**—those reliant on conquest-driven extraction with limited reinvestment—contrast sharply with **productive empires**, which invest in infrastructure and institutions to spur endogenous growth. Drawing on Mancur Olson's **stationary bandit theory**, tribute systems encourage elite rent-seeking, fostering fiscal fragility and revolts, while Douglass North's **institutional economics** highlights how productive strategies align principals and agents through productivity-enhancing rules [2, 3]. Applied to Rome, Spain, and France, this lens reveals extraction's inverse link to durability, modulated by geopolitical pressures per Besley and Persson's **selectorate theory**, where war finance shapes reinvestment [7]. Confounders like plagues and geography complicate causation, yet cliometric models (e.g., vector autoregression on fiscal flows) underscore economic weights, with endogeneity addressed via instrumental variables like exogenous silver discoveries [18, 44].

Rome's trajectory from Republic to Empire (509 BCE–476 CE West) leaned heavily tribute-based, with provincial **stipendium** taxes and slave inflows funding an Italian core [8]. Elite consolidation of **latifundia**—senators holding 15–25% of arable land—prioritized slave rents over innovation, per Cicero's critiques, amid path-dependent fiscal loops where conquests (e.g., Asia Minor yields post-133 BCE) disincentivized domestic investment [9, 10, 11]. Provincial merchants and freedmen drove some trade innovation (e.g., via client-patron networks), but selectorate pressures—small winning coalitions of senators—locked in extraction, ignoring sub-elite agency [45, 46]. Soil exhaustion and debasement (silver halved by 235 CE) fueled the Third Century Crisis, with economic factors at 40–50% causality alongside plagues (Cyprian, 250 CE) and climate shifts [14, 15, 47].
